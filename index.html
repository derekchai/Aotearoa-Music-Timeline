The Dunedin Sound and its Legacy

Chronologising Ōtepoti's 1980s alternative rock history

by DEREK CHAI for MUS149G

Cover image by Mia Rankin

The "Dunedin Sound" is a term for a movement cultural as much as it was musical, emerging in the 1980s and a defining factor of Dunedin's music scene. While potentially equivocal in its existence (Mills 2021, 19–20) and certainly overwhelmingly white and male-dominated (Bannister 2006, xiii), it nevertheless cannot be denied that the movement has had a lasting impact on the Southern city.

Hallmarks of the so-called "Sound" include jangly guitars and droning notes (Schmidt 2016), and the subgenre itself, born from punk and post-punk, is regarded to have spurred on the development of adjacent genres such as alternative and indie rock.

The formation of the Dunedin Sound is attributed to the Southern city's isolation (Chapman 2024, 20) almost as much as this same claim is refuted (Schmidt 2016).
Regardless of the Sound's actual existence, which musician and Performing Arts senior lecturer at the Universty of Otago Ian Chapman argues is "pointless to debate [...] ad nauseum" (Chapman 2024, 20), the physical distance between Ōtepoti and the cultural centres of Western music in the UK and US by which the movement was so influenced cannot be denied.
Funnily enough, the punk movement, the Dunedin Sound's predecessor, found its New Zealand roots in Auckland (Russell 2017, 2). Imported from the punk revolution in Europe (from the UK especially so), 1977 saw the formation of Auckland's, and indeed New Zealand's first two punk bands: The Scavengers, and the Suburban Reptiles (Grigg 2019), formed at Auckland Technical Institute (now Auckland University of Technology) and the University of Auckland, respectively.
At the heart of Auckland's punk scene was the nightclub Zwines. Located on Durham Lane off Queen Street in the Auckland CBD (Shute 2023), it was a nexus for the punk and youth subculture from its opening in 1978 before its interior was ravaged by fire in 1979 (Shute 2023).

The Dunedin Sound shared a lot of similarities with the punk movement. The countercultural anti-establishmentarianism of the scene was a direct attack upon "artifice" (Robertson 1991, 18) and the pre-existing " 'muso' scene" (Knox, quoted in Schmidt 2013b) and these notions would eventually give way to the DIY ethos prevailing within the Dunedin Sound (Robertson 1991, 19).

However, punk manifested itself in vastly different ways in Dunedin and Auckland. While voguish and fast-paced Auckland was more accepting of the fashion and cultural trends coming from New Zealand's antipodean centres, Dunedinite attitudes were more cynical to these emerging concepts (Robertson 1991, 21), and so, the divergence from punk and the formation of the Dunedin Sound.

Influenced by the sound of the Beatles and the Sex Pistols (Schmidt 2013b), the Enemy was formed in 1977 (Robertson 1991, 14). Being Dunedin's first punk group (Schmidt 2013b), they are a pivotal band in the countercultural punk scene in Ōtepoti, and they are arguably at the forefront of the beginning of the Dunedin Sound itself.

◂ The Enemy (Reg Feuz, 1978).

Soon after the formation of The Enemy came a slew of new Dunedin bands, one of which was The Clean, which would eventually go on to be one of the most influential bands of the entire scene. Formed in 1978, the band's original lineup comprised of brothers Hamish and David Kilgour, and David's friend Peter Gutteridge (Robertson 1991, 22–23). Their debut single Tally Ho charted for 7 weeks, peaking at #19 (Robertson 1991, 153), and has become one of the most iconic songs to have come from the genre.

◂ The Clean in Auckland Star (Auckland Star, 1979).

1979 saw the (disasterous) debut of The Same (Robertson 1991, 24) at the iconic Dunedin Beneficiaries Hall, which too hosted the debut of The Clean and a plethora of Enemy performances (Mills 2022). Vocalist/guitarist Martin Phillipps of The Same would later go on to form The Chills in 1980 (Robertson 1991, 32).

◂ The Same at Logan Park High School (Jane Dodd, 1980).

Bored Games, definitively punk, debuted at Kaikorai Valley High School's school talent quest in 1979. While the band itself lasted less than two years, it left a legacy in the form of Shayne Carter's Straitjacket Fits 7 years later (AudioCulture 2019).

◂ Bored Games in 1981 (Lesley Paris, 1981).

And so, The Clean, The Same, Toy Love (The Enemy reincarnated), and Bored Games form the so-called "first wave" of the Dunedin Sound (Robertson 1991, 22). These bands among others would go on to play at venues across Dunedin, further cementing this emerging musical scene within the city.

Dunedin bands played venues across the city. Venues represented not only a place to play but also a place for the community to get together, united by a common love for music.
Today, many of these historic venues remain and have been restored, and some continue to host Dunedin bands to this day.

These initial bands paved the way for the second-wave bands of the Dunedin Sound—in fact, many were formed after seeing the performances of these pioneering acts.

The Verlaines was formed in 1980 after frontman Graeme Downes saw the performances of The Clean and The Same in 1979 (Robertson 1991, 33), and references to high culture were not just in their name but also the music itself—within the sleeve notes of 1985 Flying Nun compilation album Tuatara, the late renowned Dunedin record-store owner Roy Colbert (McConnell 2017) writes: a Verlaines song is "ached over in composition" and "set fire to live" (Bendrups 2008, 2–8). This is not more clearly seen than in Verlaines single "Death and the Maiden".

◂ Graeme Downes, Jane Dodd, Greg Kerr of The Verlaines (Terry Moore, 1982).

Sneaky Feelings sounds different to their contemporaries. The band was formed in 1980 with the original line-up of Matthew Bannister, David Pine, Kathryn Tyrie, and Martin Durrant​ (Robertson 1991, 148). Their sound is distinctively psychedelic and dreamy (Dass 2017), in stark contrast to the high-energy post-punk sound prevalent in other bands such as the works of The Clean or The Stones (Robertson 1991, 43). This unique, slow-paced sound is exemplified in moody ballad "Husband House" on their first album Send You (1983, Flying Nun), which peaked at #19, charting for 7 weeks (Robertson 1991, 155).

◂ Sneaky Feelings c. 1983: David Pine, Kathryn Tyrie, Matthew Bannister and Martin Durrant (Simon Grigg collection, 1983).

The Chills are another one of the great bands to come out of the Dunedin Sound. Formed from ex-members of The Same in 1980 and fronted by Martin Phillipps, they played and toured internationally while maintaining their success and popularity at home (Schmidt 2024), and to this day they are one of the bands of the time who continue to release new music. Martin Phillipps passed away in 2024 (Burns 2024).

◂ The Chills at Captain Cook Tavern (Gordon Bartram, 1984).

With its lineup including former members of bands Bored Games and The Same, continuing the trend of former band members going on to form new bands, The Stones was formed in the winter of 1980 ​ (Robertson 1991, 33). While perhaps not as well-known as their contemporaries and relatively short-lived, they still are an important part of the scene. The 1982 Dunedin Double EP (Flying Nun) features The Stones' "Surf's Up", a dig at surf culture dripping with sarcasm (Schmidt 2013c).

◂ The Stones at the Bowl of Brooklands, New Plymouth (Glen Tucker, 1983).

It's impossible to talk about the Dunedin Sound without mentioning Flying Nun Records. While so influential in Dunedin's music history, the label was actually founded in Christchurch 1981 by Roger Shepherd (Stafford 2017) and signed a large majority of the bands of the era. The aforementioned Dunedin Double (1982) EP and later compilation album Tuarara (1985) are a Who's Who of the era's most influential bands, and some of their most iconic numbers.

◂ Roger Shepherd in the Flying Nun offices; Christchurch 1987 (Alec Bathgate, 1987).

The Bats was formed in 1982 from ex-members of (now disbanded) The Clean and Toy. Their longevity in the scene is exhibited by their constant line-up since their inception until this day (where they still continue to make music and perform)​ (Higgins and Howells 2021). Their discography comprises infectiously-upbeat jangle pop, such as the title track on their 1986 EP Made Up In Blue. They are another Dunedin band who has managed to find success overseas, with international tours across the United States, Europe, and Australia (The Bats 2015).

◂ The Bats publicity shot in Hagley Park, Christchurch (Flying Nun, 1995).

Look Blue Go Purple formed in 1983. The fact of their gender "followed the five-piece wherever they went" to their justified frustration (Stafford 2017)—all-male groups certainly weren't endlessly referred to as such (Radio New Zealand Music 101 2024) but it is certainly true that Look Blue Go Purple went against the grain in such an incredibly male-dominated scene. Regardless, they certainly have left a lasting legacy in inspiring young women in the music industry (Radio New Zealand Music 101 2024). Folksy "Cactus Cat" on their sophomore EP LBGPEP2 (Flying Nun, 1986) is a carefree summery pop tune, accompanied by a music video featuring cats and cacti aplenty (O'Neill 1986).

◂ Look Blue Go Purple, Dunedin, 1985. (L-R) Denise Roughan, Kathy Bull, Lesley Paris, Norma O'Malley, Kath Webster (Jeremy Freeman, 1985).

If there was one thing the Dunedin Sound did, it was encouraging local radio stations to finally play local music, and at the forefront was student radio. University of Otago student radio station Radio One was established in 1984 (Radio One 91FM n.d.) and was prominent in playing local acts. Dunedin musician Stephen Kilroy notes how Radio One were "instrumental in making the whole scene take off" (Radio New Zealand 2022).

◂ Radio One original logo 1985–90 (Radio One, 1985).

These bands are just some of the many which make up the successive "waves" of the Dunedin Sound, of which there is no precise definition. Certainly, the early to mid-1980s were the heyday of the Sound. From then on, the movement began to become less centralised and cohesive as bands disbanded, such as The Clean in June 1982 (Robertson 1991, 75), members pursued careers or education, and others moved intercity or internationally.

Typical New Zealand tall poppy syndrome was rampart in the community as well—this is exhibited in The Chills' decision to move to Auckland in 1984 to try for success in the Big Smoke, while also acting as a stepping stone for their eventual international career, which garnered accusations of selling out and falling into the "commercial trap" (Robertson 1991, 77–9).

We're starting to cop a bit of flak, but it's not our problem, it's people's attitudes... it's a bit naïve, really, thinking we're forever going to be a garage band that just plays at the Empire. We've been offered the opportunity to make a real go of it and we're going to take it.

The Chills in the Otago Daily Times 1984.

Straitjacket Fits was formed in 1986 from the demise of the short-lived DoubleHappys in 1985 (Robertson 1991, 8), which itself was formed from members of Shayne Carter's Bored Games. Straitjacket Fits' sound diverges from the jangle and drone, cornerstones of the original Dunedin Sound (Mills 2016a)—their 1992 EP Done and its Top 20 title track bears little resemblance to the sounds of the Fits' predecessors (Mills 2016b). However, they are one of the few bands emerging from the scene who managed to garner success abroad (Radio New Zealand Music 2005), including getting signed to American record label Arista Records in one of the largest deals for a New Zealand group at the time (Robertson 1991, 139).

Straitjacket Fits' first gig at Chippendale House, 1986 (Jeff Hartford, 1986).

If Straitjacket Fits had diverged from the original Dunedin Sound, The Dead C—formed in 1987 (Beat Magazine 2010)—did a complete U-turn on the genre. The Dead C's sound is highly experimental, with elements of noise rock and experimental post-rock (Jorgensen 2016, 315). Regardless of their seeming "incompetence" and "intentional amateurism", they achieved moderate international success (Jorgensen 2016, 316–8) including cover in international music magazines (Jorgensen 2016, 318) and performances in Europe and the United States (Schmidt 2013a), and the band continues to be active today.

The Dead C at The Empire Tavern, Dundedin, 1998 (The Dead C, 1998).

Despite being a band full of Aucklanders (Chapman 2013), The 3Ds formed in Dunedin in 1988 and would become one of the most popular acts of Flying Nun's third wave of bands (Otago Daily Times 2021). Their sophomore album The Venus Trail (1993, Flying Nun) achieved critical success on New Zealand music charts, charting in the top 10, and their musical career would see them tour with and support bands the likes of Pavement, U2 (Otago Daily Times 2021), and Nirvana (Shea 2014).

The 3Ds in 1993: (L-R) Dominic Stones, David Mitchell, David Saunders and Denise Roughan (Darryl Ward, 1993).

The Straitjacket Fits' Andrew Brough would go on to form Bike in 1992​ (National Library of New Zealand n.d.). Stephen Kilroy notes the band's "Take in the Sun", the first track off their 1997 album of the same name, "typefies the 'Dunedin Sound' " (Radio New Zealand 2022), and played a part in the band's nomination of the New Zealand Music Awards' "most promising new band" in 1997 (Schmidt 2020). Take in the Sun, filled with surging summery ballads, would peak at #47 on local charts in October of the same year (Aotearoa Music Charts n.d.). Bike would end up being relatively short-lived and release only a small amount of music. Andrew Brough retreated from the public spotlight after the release of Take in the Sun and passed away in 2020 at age 56 (Reid 2021).
High Dependency Unit formed in 1994 by Neil Phillips, Tristan Dingemans, and Dino Karlis, all of whom hailed from Hawke's Bay but would eventually move to Dunedin (McIver 2025). Like The Dead C, their sound is similarly noisy and psychedelic, and the act have been described as "distorted, dangerous and often devastating" by Rolling Stone and "one of the 10 best bands in the world you have never heard of" by BBC Radio 1 (Copek 2025). They continue to perform and will tour the country in November 2025 (Cudby 2025).
Directed by Robert Sarkies, Scarfies was released in 1999 by Becker Entertainment and stars Willa O'Neill, Taika Cohen (Taika Waititi), Jon Brazier, Neill Rea, Ashleigh Seagar, and Charlie Bleakley. Set in Dunedin, the film is centered across the unexpected discovery of a marijuana crop in the basement of an abandoned flat by five Otago University students and the events which unfold as a result. The soundtrack of the film features prominent Dunedin Sound tunes, including Save My Life (Bike), Tally Ho (The Clean), Cactus Cat (Look Blue Go Purple), and Death and the Maiden (The Verlaines), as well as a cameo performance by The Clean playing themselves (Sarkies 1999). Scarfies was well-received, including winning Best Film, Best Director, and Best Female Actor at the 2000 Nokia New Zealand Film Awards (Sandy 2000).
