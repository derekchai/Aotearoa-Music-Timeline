Dunedin Sound

A History of Ōtepoti's Music Culture

by DEREK CHAI for MUS149G

Cover image by Mia Rankin

The "Dunedin Sound" is a term for a movement cultural as much as it was musical, emerging in the 1980s and a defining factor of Dunedin's music scene. While potentially equivocal in its existence (Mills 2021, 19–20) and certainly overwhelmingly white and male-dominated (Bannister 2006, xiii), it nevertheless cannot be denied that the movement has had a lasting impact on the Southern city.

Hallmarks of the so-called "Sound" include jangly guitars and droning notes (Schmidt 2016), and the subgenre itself, born from punk and post-punk, is regarded to have spurred on the development of adjacent genres such as alternative and indie rock.

The formation of the Dunedin Sound is attributed to the Southern city's isolation (Chapman 2024, 20) almost as much as this claim is refuted (Schmidt 2016).
Regardless of the Sound's actual existence, which musician and Performing Arts senior lecturer at the Universty of Otago Ian Chapman argues is "pointless to debate [...] ad nauseum" (Chapman 2024, 20), the physical distance between Ōtepoti and the cultural centres of Western music in the UK and US by which the movement was so influenced cannot be denied.
