Dunedin Sound

Chronologising Ōtepoti's 1980s Music Culture

by DEREK CHAI for MUS149G

Cover image by Mia Rankin

The "Dunedin Sound" is a term for a movement cultural as much as it was musical, emerging in the 1980s and a defining factor of Dunedin's music scene. While potentially equivocal in its existence (Mills 2021, 19–20) and certainly overwhelmingly white and male-dominated (Bannister 2006, xiii), it nevertheless cannot be denied that the movement has had a lasting impact on the Southern city.

Hallmarks of the so-called "Sound" include jangly guitars and droning notes (Schmidt 2016), and the subgenre itself, born from punk and post-punk, is regarded to have spurred on the development of adjacent genres such as alternative and indie rock.

The formation of the Dunedin Sound is attributed to the Southern city's isolation (Chapman 2024, 20) almost as much as this same claim is refuted (Schmidt 2016).
Regardless of the Sound's actual existence, which musician and Performing Arts senior lecturer at the Universty of Otago Ian Chapman argues is "pointless to debate [...] ad nauseum" (Chapman 2024, 20), the physical distance between Ōtepoti and the cultural centres of Western music in the UK and US by which the movement was so influenced cannot be denied.
Funnily enough, the punk movement, the Dunedin Sound's predecessor, found its New Zealand roots in Auckland (Russell 2017, 2). Imported from the punk revolution in Europe (from the UK especially so), 1977 saw the formation of Auckland's, and indeed New Zealand's first two punk bands: The Scavengers, and the Suburban Reptiles (Grigg 2019), formed at Auckland Technical Institute (now Auckland University of Technology) and the University of Auckland, respectively.
At the heart of Auckland's punk scene was the nightclub Zwines. Located on Durham Lane off Queen Street in the Auckland CBD (Shute 2023), it was a nexus for the punk and youth subculture from its opening in 1978 before its interior was ravaged by fire in 1979 (Shute 2023).

The Dunedin Sound shared a lot of similarities with the punk movement. The countercultural anti-establishmentarianism of the scene was a direct attack upon "artifice" (Robertson 1991, 18) and the pre-existing " 'muso' scene" (Knox, quoted in Schmidt 2013a) and these notions would eventually give way to the DIY ethos prevailing within the Dunedin Sound (Robertson 1991, 19).

However, punk manifested itself in vastly different ways in Dunedin and Auckland. While voguish and fast-paced Auckland was more accepting of the fashion and cultural trends coming from New Zealand's antipodean centres, Dunedinite attitudes were more cynical to these emerging concepts (Robertson 1991, 21), and so, the divergence from punk and the formation of the Dunedin Sound.

Influenced by the sound of the Beatles and the Sex Pistols (Schmidt 2013a), the Enemy was formed in 1977 (Robertson 1991, 14). Being Dunedin's first punk group (Schmidt 2013a), they are a pivotal band in the countercultural punk scene in Ōtepoti, and they are arguably at the forefront of the beginning of the Dunedin Sound itself.

◂ The Enemy (Reg Feuz, 1978).

Soon after the formation of The Enemy came a slew of new Dunedin bands, one of which was The Clean, which would eventually go on to be one of the most influential bands of the entire scene. Formed in 1978, the band's original lineup comprised of brothers Hamish and David Kilgour, and David's friend Peter Gutteridge (Robertson 1991, 22–23). Their debut single Tally Ho charted for 7 weeks, peaking at #19 (Robertson 1991, 153), and has become one of the most iconic songs to have come from the genre.

◂ The Clean in Auckland Star (Auckland Star, 1979).

1979 saw the (disasterous) debut of The Same (Robertson 1991, 24) at the iconic Dunedin Beneficiaries Hall, which too hosted the debut of The Clean and a plethora of Enemy performances (Mills 2022). Vocalist/guitarist Martin Phillipps of The Same would later go on to form The Chills in 1980 (Robertson 1991, 32).

◂ The Same at Logan Park High School (Jane Dodd, 1980).

Bored Games, definitively punk, debuted at Kaikorai Valley High School's school talent quest in 1979. While the band itself lasted less than two years, it left a legacy in the form of Shayne Carter's Straitjacket Fits 7 years later (AudioCulture 2019).

◂ Bored Games in 1981 (Lesley Paris, 1981).

And so, The Clean, The Same, Toy Love (The Enemy reincarnated), and Bored Games form the so-called "first wave" of the Dunedin Sound (Robertson 1991, 22). These bands among others would go on to play at venues across Dunedin, further cementing this emerging musical scene within the city.
Dunedin bands played venues across the city. Venues represented not only a place to play but also a place for the community to get together, united by a common love for music.
Today, many of these historic venues remain and have been restored, and some continue to host Dunedin bands to this day.
These initial bands paved the way for the second-wave bands of the Dunedin Sound—in fact, many were formed after seeing the performances of these pioneering acts.

The Verlaines was formed in 1980 after frontman Graeme Downes saw the performances of The Clean and The Same in 1979 (Robertson 1991, 33), and references to high culture were not just in their name but also the music itself—within the sleeve notes of 1985 Flying Nun compilation album Tuatara, the late renowned Dunedin record-store owner Roy Colbert (McConnell 2017) writes: a Verlaines song is "ached over in composition" and "set fire to live" (Bendrups 2008, 2–8). This is not more clearly seen than in Verlaines single "Death and the Maiden".
The Chills are another one of the great bands to come out of the Dunedin Sound. Formed from ex-members of The Same in 1980 and fronted by Martin Phillipps, they played and toured internationally while maintaining their success and popularity at home (Schmidt 2024), and to this day they continue to create music. Phillipps died in 2024 (Burns 2024).
With its lineup including former members of bands Bored Games and The Same, continuing the trend of former band members going on to form new bands, The Stones was formed in the winter of 1980 ​ (Robertson 1991, 33). The 1982 Dunedin Double EP (Flying Nun) features The Stones' "Surf's Up", a dig at surf culture dripping with sarcasm (Schmidt 2013b).
